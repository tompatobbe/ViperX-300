A B S T R A C T
The demand for novel robotic applications continues to grow, driven by the need for advanced solutions to
complex tasks. These tasks often require the incorporation of dynamic models into the development of control
schemes. Consequently, model identification has become a critical step in creating accurate dynamic models
for robotic arms. However, the identification process can yield parameters that lack physical significance,
resulting in feasibility issues that lead to unrealistic and potentially unstable dynamic models. This challenge is
particularly pronounced in small robotic arms, where the dynamics are highly sensitive to parameter variations.
To address these challenges, this article presents a systematic and comprehensive approach to the model
identification of robotic arms while strictly enforcing physical feasibility. The proposed approach is validated
on the ViperX-300 6DoF robotic manipulator, which lacks a pre-existing dynamic model, making it an ideal
candidate for testing our method. To further validate the proposed model identification approach and assess the
suitability of the obtained model for the ViperX-300 6DoF robotic manipulator in safety-critical applications,
we design an explicit reference governor, which is a model-based constrained control strategy. Experimental
results show that the identified model achieves sufficient accuracy for safety-critical applications, showcasing
the effectiveness of the proposed model identification approach and the reliability of the identified model for
the ViperX-300 6DoF robotic manipulator.
1. Introduction
Robotic arms, also known as robotic manipulators, are increasingly
employed across various industries to perform tasks requiring high
precision and performance, such as arc welding, machining, packaging,
assembly, laser welding, and laser cutting. Achieving the required
performance for these applications necessitates advanced control techniques, which often rely on an accurate understanding of the robot’s
dynamic behavior. However, many robot manufacturers either do not
provide a complete dynamic model or offer only limited details about
certain parameters and aspects. Consequently, dynamic modeling of
robotic arms has become a critical research focus in the robotics field,
as highlighted in studies such as [1–5].
Dynamic modeling of robotic arms entails understanding the underlying relationship between joint motion and the forces or torques
applied to each joint. A common approach to model this relationship
is to use supervised learning techniques, such as linear regression,
which can be viewed as both a statistical and machine learning method
depending on the context. In this approach, the robot’s dynamic model
is identified using data on joint positions, velocities, accelerations, and

Despite its effectiveness, dynamic models identified through regression techniques often face challenges related to physical infeasibility, as
demonstrated in [11–14]. Specifically, such models may lack physical
consistency or fail to adhere to fundamental laws of physics, such
as the conservation of energy or accurate representation of inertial
properties. For example, parameters like mass, inertia, or damping
may take unrealistic or nonsensical values, resulting in models that are
not physically valid. This limitation can significantly compromise the

This paper is motivated by the limitations and challenges outlined
above and aims to present a systematic and comprehensive procedure
for the closed-loop identification of robotic arms, ensuring strict guarantees of physical feasibility. To guide readers through this procedure,
we provide a step-by-step application on the ViperX-300 6DoF robotic
manipulator [20]. To validate and evaluate the proposed procedure, we
compare the identified model with actual robot data across two different trajectories. Additionally, following the recommendations of prior
work [21–23], we evaluate the performance of the identified model
by implementing a model-based constrained control scheme derived
from it. For this purpose, we first design a robust control system and
provide theoretical guarantees for stability and tracking convergence.
This control law is structured to facilitate the design of the constrainthandling controller. Next, we employ the Explicit Reference Governor
(ERG) [24–28], a model-based add-on unit that augments the control
system with constraint-handling capabilities. Our experimental results
and analysis indicate that the identified model is sufficiently accurate
for tracking purposes and safety-critical applications, demonstrating the
effectiveness of the proposed procedure and the reliability of the identified model for the ViperX-300 6DoF robotic manipulator. The resulting
model is made available at [29] to facilitate its use by researchers in
their studies involving the ViperX-300 6DoF robotic manipulator.
The key contributions of this paper are as follows: (i) the presentation of a systematic, step-by-step approach for identifying physically
feasible dynamic models of robotic arms, demonstrated through experimental validation on the ViperX-300 6-DoF manipulator, which
currently lacks a reliable dynamic model; (ii) the development of a
novel robust control system for robotic arms, along with a comprehensive analysis of its stability and tracking performance; and (iii) the
development and experimental validation of an ERG for the constrained
control of robotic arms subject to joint constraints. Note that the ERG
scheme outlined in [30–32] for controlling robotic arms depends on
explicit predictions of the robot trajectory, making it potentially impractical for real-time applications. Additionally, the efforts in [33,34]
are confined to simulation studies, leaving their applicability to realworld robots questionable. To the best of our knowledge, this paper
represents the first attempt to experimentally apply the ERG framework
to ensure joint constraints in robotic arms without necessitating explicit
predictions of the system’s trajectory.
The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents
the closed-loop identification method and discusses approaches to ensure the physical feasibility of the identified model. Section 3 applies
the developed procedure step-by-step to the ViperX-300 6DoF robotic
manipulator, reporting the obtained experimental results. In Section 4,
an ERG-based control scheme is designed, and the experimental results
from its application to the ViperX-300 6DoF robotic manipulator are
reported. Finally, Section 5 concludes this paper.

2.2. Regression model
In this section, we employ a regression-based method to explicitly
identify the 𝑀, 𝐶, and 𝐺 matrices in Eq. (1), ensuring physical interpretability and enabling direct integration into model-based control,
stability analysis, and adaptive schemes. These methods are computationally efficient, transparent, and well-suited for applications where a
structured model is required, though they can be sensitive to noise and
necessitate sufficiently rich system excitation for accurate parameter
estimation. In contrast, physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) [38–
41] embed physical laws into a neural network framework, providing
flexibility, strong generalization to unseen conditions, and inherent
enforcement of physical consistency without explicit matrix formulations. However, PINNs typically produce black-box representations
rather than structured dynamics, complicating integration into classical
control frameworks.

Remark 2. It is important to note that the positive definiteness of the
mass matrix 𝑀(𝑞) was not enforced explicitly as a constraint in the
optimization problem (16). Enforcing this property during optimization
would require gridding the entire joint configuration space at a fine
resolution, evaluating 𝑀(𝑞) at each grid point, and imposing a positive

result in an extremely large number of constraints, significantly increasing the computational burden and making the optimization intractable
for a 6-DoF manipulator. Even then, full physical consistency of the
system would still not be guaranteed.
Remark 3. While the optimization problem in (16) remains tractable
for the 6-DoF robotic arm considered in this study, we acknowledge
that the computational complexity of enforcing physical feasibility constraints can increase substantially for higher-DoF systems. Several prior
works [15,53] have investigated methods to address this challenge.
Although scalability is not the primary focus of this article, future work
will explore strategies to enhance the efficiency and applicability of the
proposed identification method for high-DoF robotic platforms.

3.3. Data collection
To identify the manipulator parameters, training data is collected
on applied torques, joint positions, velocities, and accelerations. While
the robot’s sensors directly measure positions and velocities, accelerations are not directly measured. Thus, to generate the training data,
acceleration is first derived from velocity measurements using the Euler
method (i.e., the first-order forward difference approximation), and the
resulting signal is then processed with a zero-phase filtering technique
using a low-pass filter with a cutoff frequency of 10 Hz. This zerophase filtering can be implemented in MATLAB using the filtfilt
function.
3.4. Identification
Once the data on joint angles and applied torques is collected, we
use the optimization problem (16) to identify the base parameters.
Note that, according to the frames shown in Fig. 1, constraint (16i)
is implemented as 𝑚2𝑋2 > 0, 𝑚3𝑌3 > 0, 𝑚4𝑍4 < 0, 𝑚5𝑌5 > 0,

associated row of the regression matrix in (9), was scaled by the
maximum absolute torque observed for that joint over all samples.

3.5. Feasibility check
Since constraints on mass, first mass moments, friction coefficients,
and inertia matrices are explicitly enforced within the optimization
problem (16), ensuring physical feasibility reduces to verifying the
positive definiteness of the mass matrix 𝑀(𝑞) across the entire operating range defined by the joint angle and velocity limits. To this end,
the operating range of each joint angle was discretized in one-degree
increments, and the corresponding mass matrix 𝑀(𝑞) was evaluated at
each point. In all cases, 𝑀(𝑞) remained positive definite, confirming
that the kinetic energy is non-negative throughout the operating range.
These results rigorously demonstrate that the identified dynamic model
is physically feasible.

then formatted and transmitted back to the ROS2 node for real-time
control calculations. All the code used in this experimental study is

5. Conclusion
This paper presented a systematic and comprehensive procedure
for dynamics identification of robotic manipulators. It also addressed
ensuring physical feasibility, despite the varying torque ranges across
different joints in these robots. The proposed procedure was validated
on the ViperX-300 6DoF robotic manipulator, as no dynamic model
has been derived for this robotic arm. Additionally, a novel robust
control scheme was developed for robotic arms with model uncertainty, and its stability and tracking performance guarantees were
derived analytically. To enhance the constraint satisfaction capabilities
of the developed control scheme, we developed an explicit reference
governor. This model-based add-on unit modifies the reference signal
as needed to ensure that constraints on joint angles are satisfied at
all times. Consequently, its effectiveness can be utilized to further
validate the identification procedure and evaluate the suitability of the
identified model for safety-critical applications. Experimental results
demonstrated that the proposed procedure successfully produces a
model with adequate accuracy for safety-critical applications and that
the identified model for the ViperX-300 6-DoF robotic manipulator is
reliable enough for further use by other researchers.
